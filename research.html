Research

My research interests are at the intersection of Behavioral and Development Economics, with a focus on social networks. I plan to empirically study how social networks and behavioral factors influence learning, decision-making, risk taking, risk sharing, and people's valuation of social capital. In particular, I'm interested in how people's behavior and beliefs are a function of interaction with and (potentially misspecified) learning from others.

In low-income, developing countries where people often have high exposure to risk, enjoy little social insurance from governments, and lack access to credit, these questions have significant welfare consequences as people tend to rely more heavily on each other and behavioral "mistakes" can cause people to forego investments with relatively high rates of return. Focusing on resource-scarce settings, my research aims to clarify how and when these social behaviors can improve well-being, how we can build on existing informal systems to improve well-being, and how we can minimize the negative welfare impacts of these behavioral "mistakes."

Applying structural methods to experimental data, I will seek to identify the underlying mechanisms that determine behavior, quantify welfare impacts, and conduct counterfactual analyses.


Works In Progress

Risk Sharing and Portfolio Choice by A. Jahić

In developing countries, lack of access to formal credit markets leads many social networks to engage in informal borrowing. This study investigages how risk sharing impacts investment behavior and risk taking.

Complexity of Labor Choice in Developing Countries by A. Jahić

Due to uncertainty in labor demand for a given job type, workers in developing countries often split their time between several job types. I investigate how the complexity of this allocation of hours to job types impacts labor outcomes.

Policy Briefs

Building to Last: Impacts of a Philadelphia Energy-Efficiency Housing Rehabilitation Program for Low-Income Households by S. Berkouwer and A. Jahić
Kleiman Center for Energy Policy, 2024

Philadelphia’s Built to Last program helps low-income homeowners navigate the complexities of home improvement assistance programs. This brief examines the early outcomes of Built to Last, three years later.
