Research

My research interests are at the intersection of Behavioral and Development Economics, with a focus on social networks. I plan to use structural tools to study how social networks and behavioral factors — such as social/altruistic preferences, bounded rationality, cognitive biases, status signalling, mental health, cultural norms, and more — influence decision-making, risk-taking, risk sharing, learning, and people's valuation of their own social capital in low-income, developing countries. Using structural econometric modelling, it is possible to identify the underlying mechanisms that determine behavior, quantify their impacts under certain conditions, and conduct counterfactual analyses.

For example, the risk sharing literature (Fafchamps, 2011) shows that households and networks often distribute risk between each other and give or loan money to others in their social networks, so one person’s economic outcomes often affect other connected people. I want to advance this literature by considering how information — which may be processed with bias — from or about other people in their network influences people's consumption, saving, risk-taking, and risk-sharing behavior. If members of a social network change their consumption, saving, and risk-taking behavior after hearing about or observing one person’s income change, that may affect the well-being of the entire network. If network members increase their consumption or risk-taking knowing they could be "bailed out" ex-post, this would be a form of a moral hazard problem. It is also possible that the network members' change in behaviors may maximize aggregate welfare and lead to optimal risk sharing. Understanding the conditions that lead to positive vs. negative network-level outcomes and how networks can optimize aggregate risk can improve the lives of people in the developing countries.

Policy Articles

Building to Last: Impacts of a Philadelphia Energy-Efficiency Housing Rehabilitation Program for Low-Income Households by S. Berkouwer and A. Jahić
Kleiman Center for Energy Policy, 2024

Philadelphia’s Built to Last program helps low-income homeowners navigate the complexities of home improvement assistance programs. This working paper examines the early outcomes of Built to Last, three years later.
